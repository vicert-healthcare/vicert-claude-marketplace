## Start Here — What's Their Primary Pain?

| They Say Something Like… | The Underlying Problem | Recommend |
|---------------------------|------------------------|-----------|
| "We're paying for five different systems and they don't talk to each other" / "We have people whose whole job is copying data between platforms" / "Our vendor keeps promising features that never come" | Patchwork of SaaS tools that each cover 60–70% of needs, with manual labor filling the gaps | **Custom Healthcare Platform** |
| "If Janet leaves, we're in trouble" / "Our auditors would have a field day with this spreadsheet" / "We tried to replace this process before but the quote was $800K" | Business-critical processes running on shadow systems — spreadsheets, Access DBs, tribal knowledge | **Workflow Automation** |
| "Our exception queue just keeps growing" / "We can't write rules fast enough for all the variations" / "We know there are patterns in our denials but we can't see them" | Work that requires judgment, not just rules — traditional software breaks on healthcare's complexity | **AI Agent Systems** |
| "We can't hire engineers fast enough" / "Our roadmap is 6 months behind" / "We need healthcare domain expertise we can't find" / "Our investors want us to ship, not recruit" | Technology company building a healthcare product but constrained by engineering capacity, healthcare domain expertise gap, or time-to-market pressure | **Health-Tech Engagement** |

## Signal Checklist

### Custom Healthcare Platforms — Look for:
- Spending $200K+/year on SaaS tools that overlap or leave gaps
- Staff dedicated to reconciling data between systems
- Compliance processes held together by email and spreadsheets
- Previous custom development quotes of $1M+ that killed the project
- Vendor features "on the roadmap" for 2+ years
- Claims or eligibility workflows with manual steps
- Annual SaaS price increases causing budget pressure

### Workflow Automation — Look for:
- Excel files or Access databases that are "business-critical"
- Processes that depend on specific people (key-person risk)
- Onboarding takes months because nothing is documented
- Audit prep causes anxiety because the tracking is manual
- "Temporary" processes that have been running for 5+ years
- Teams spending >50% of time on data wrangling vs. decisions
- Recent or upcoming retirement of a key process owner

### AI Agent Systems — Look for:
- Exception queues that are large and growing
- Document processing with meaningful format variation
- Automation projects delayed because specification effort is too large
- Staff spending significant time interpreting, not just processing
- Denial patterns that nobody can see across the full data set
- Edge cases that multiply faster than rules can be written
- Existing automation (RPA, rules engines) that's hitting diminishing returns
- Quality measure abstraction is expensive, seasonal, and limited to statistical samples
- Provider directory accuracy is a compliance concern (CMS mandates, state requirements)
- Clinical documentation quality is assessed manually on small samples only
- Chart review is a time sink for clinicians (10+ minutes per patient in EHR navigation)
- Organization has HEDIS/Stars obligations with room for measure capture improvement

### Health-Tech Engagement — Look for:
- Founding team with healthcare domain knowledge but limited engineering capacity
- Open engineering roles unfilled for 3+ months
- Product roadmap significantly behind schedule due to staffing gaps
- Series A–C funding with pressure to show product traction before next raise
- Need for healthcare-specific expertise (HIPAA, FHIR, adjudication logic) that the current team lacks
- Previous attempts with offshore development that failed on healthcare domain complexity
- Technical co-founder stretched thin across architecture, coding, hiring, and management

## Segmentation by Organization Type

*The pitch changes depending on who you're talking to. Use this to calibrate.*

### Healthcare Services Organizations (HSOs)

*Includes TPAs, PBMs, clearinghouses, RCM companies, credentialing verification orgs, healthcare BPOs, and similar organizations that provide administrative, operational, or technology services to the healthcare industry.*

| Characteristic | Implications for Your Pitch |
|----------------|---------------------------|
| **Revenue model:** Varies: per-claim fees (TPAs), per-transaction (clearinghouses), per-member (PBMs), or project-based. | ROI must connect to the specific revenue driver — claims efficiency, transaction throughput, or operational margin |
| **SaaS landscape:** Typically 3–6 overlapping tools | Custom Platform is often the primary entry point |
| **Common shadow systems:** Claims reconciliation, payment tracking, credentialing, compliance reporting, provider data management | Workflow Automation strong secondary play |
| **AI readiness:** Varies widely — some are sophisticated, some are still faxing | Qualify carefully before leading with AI Agents |
| **Decision-maker:** CEO/GM or VP Operations at smaller HSOs; VP Ops + CIO at larger ones | Smaller HSOs move faster; larger ones have procurement processes |
| **Budget cycle:** Often annual, sometimes tied to client contract renewals or regulatory cycles | Align proposal timing with their budget process |

### Mid-Size Payers (Health Plans, 50K–300K Members)

| Characteristic | Implications for Your Pitch |
|----------------|---------------------------|
| **Revenue model:** Premium revenue, focus on medical loss ratio | ROI must connect to administrative cost reduction and claims accuracy |
| **SaaS landscape:** Typically a core platform (Facets, QNXT, etc.) plus bolt-ons | Custom Platform replaces bolt-ons, not the core. Be clear about this. |
| **Common shadow systems:** Credentialing, grievance tracking, compliance reporting | Workflow Automation for processes their core platform doesn't cover |
| **AI readiness:** Interested but cautious. Compliance teams are gatekeepers. | AI Agents require early compliance engagement — don't skip this |
| **Decision-maker:** VP Operations (champion) + CIO (technical approval) + CFO (budget) | Multi-stakeholder — plan for 3–5 decision-makers minimum |
| **Budget cycle:** Annual, often finalized Q3–Q4 for next year | Start conversations Q2–Q3 to get into next year's budget |

### Provider Groups / MSOs

| Characteristic | Implications for Your Pitch |
|----------------|---------------------------|
| **Revenue model:** Management fees, shared savings, practice revenue | ROI often connects to operational efficiency across multiple sites |
| **SaaS landscape:** EHR-centric (Epic, athenahealth, etc.) plus operational tools | Custom Platform for non-clinical operations; never position against the EHR |
| **Common shadow systems:** Referral management, authorization tracking, multi-site reporting | Workflow Automation is often the strongest entry point |
| **AI readiness:** Generally lower — focus is clinical, not administrative AI | Lead with Workflow Automation or Custom Platform unless they raise AI specifically |
| **Decision-maker:** COO or VP Operations; physician leadership has informal veto power | Physician buy-in matters even for operational tools — keep language non-threatening |
| **Budget cycle:** Varies; some aligned to health system parent, some independent | Ask about budget authority and approval process early |

### Specialty Plans (Behavioral, Dental, Vision, Pharmacy)

| Characteristic | Implications for Your Pitch |
|----------------|---------------------------|
| **Revenue model:** Carved-out specialty benefits, often capitated | ROI connects to adjudication accuracy and reduced manual intervention |
| **SaaS landscape:** Mainstream platforms built for medical often don't fit | Custom Platform with specialty-specific adjudication logic is highest-value |
| **Common shadow systems:** Benefit configuration workarounds, manual adjudication overrides | Workflow Automation for the workarounds around their ill-fitting core system |
| **AI readiness:** High potential — specialty claims have high variation and complexity | AI Agents for prior auth and specialty formulary management |
| **Decision-maker:** Often smaller orgs — CEO/President + VP Operations | Faster decisions, smaller budgets. Right-size the proposal. |
| **Budget cycle:** Varies | |

### Health-Tech Companies

*Includes health-tech startups, digital health companies, healthcare SaaS companies, and technology companies building products for the healthcare market. These are technology companies — their pain is engineering capacity and healthcare domain expertise, not operational process inefficiency.*

| Characteristic | Implications for Your Pitch |
|----------------|---------------------------|
| **Business model:** SaaS/platform/data product; revenue stage varies by funding (pre-revenue at Seed, early revenue at Series A, scaling at B/C) | ROI framing shifts from cost savings to time-to-market and cost-of-delay. "Every month without shipping is a month your competitors gain ground and your burn rate continues." |
| **Product stage:** Concept/MVP (Seed–A) through scaling (B/C) to mature extension (post-C/profitable) | Match the engagement to their stage: turnkey MVP build for early stage, managed delivery team for scaling, feature acceleration for mature. Don't propose a managed team to a Seed company — they need a build, not ongoing capacity. |
| **Engineering capacity:** Core pain; underhired, open roles unfilled for months, stretched technical team | Lead with the build-vs-hire math: 4 engineers = $700–900K/yr loaded cost + 6–9 months to productive. A Vicert pod starts delivering in 2 weeks. |
| **Healthcare expertise level:** Team knows software but learning healthcare (HIPAA, FHIR, adjudication logic, compliance) | Position Vicert's healthcare domain expertise as the differentiator. They can find general engineers — they can't easily find engineers who understand healthcare data, compliance, and interoperability. |
| **Decision-maker:** CTO/VP Engineering + CEO; faster decisions than healthcare orgs | Sales cycles are shorter (4–8 weeks typical). Decision-makers are technical — lead with engineering credibility, not slide decks. |
| **Budget cycle:** Funding-round-driven, not annual; post-raise is ideal timing (flush with capital, pressure to deploy it into product) | Time outreach to funding announcements. Series A/B closes are the highest-intent moment — they have capital and board pressure to show traction. |

## Combination Scenarios

Most prospects have pain that maps to one primary service. But many will benefit from a combination — especially over time. Here are common multi-service scenarios:

| Scenario | Recommended Path | Why This Order |
|----------|-----------------|----------------|
| "We have overlapping SaaS tools AND manual workflows in the gaps" | **Custom Platform** first → **Workflow Automation** | Consolidate the tools first so automated workflows have a stable foundation to connect to |
| "We've already automated the easy stuff, but exception handling is killing us" | **Workflow Automation** first → **AI Agents** | Formalize the processes so AI agents have defined workflows to plug into, not chaos |
| "We want to modernize everything" | **Custom Platform** → **Workflow Automation** → **AI Agents** | Foundation → formalized processes → intelligent automation. Don't skip steps. |
| "We just need one specific process fixed" | Usually **Workflow Automation** for a single high-pain workflow | Quick win, proves value, opens the door for bigger engagements |
| "We're drowning in prior auth paperwork" | **AI Agents** (if they have existing automation infrastructure) OR **Workflow Automation** (if they don't) | If the process itself isn't defined, you need Workflow Automation before AI can help |
| "Our vendor is terrible but we're locked in a contract" | **Workflow Automation** for the gaps now → **Custom Platform** at contract renewal | Don't wait for the vendor contract to expire — address the pain points around it |

## When the Lines Blur — Hybrid Engagements

Real prospects present problems that blur service boundaries. When a prospect doesn't fit neatly into one category, use this guidance:

### "We need a new system AND it needs AI capabilities"
**How to scope:** Single Custom Platform engagement with AI components built in. Don't force this into two separate engagements — it creates unnecessary handoff complexity and confuses the prospect. Price includes both platform and AI work. The pod handles both.

**How to position:** "This is a platform build with intelligent features. We scope it as one project, one team, one timeline."

### "We need to automate a process that involves reading varied documents"
**How to decide:** Ask two questions: (1) Is the core workflow itself well-defined? (2) Is the document variation the hard part?

- **If yes and yes:** Lead with **AI Agents**. The process flow is understood — the challenge is handling the variation. The AI agent sits inside a defined workflow.
- **If no and yes:** Lead with **Workflow Automation** and scope AI as an add-on component. You need to formalize the process before you can apply judgment to the document variation.
- **If yes and no:** Just **Workflow Automation**. The documents aren't actually that varied — they just need structured handling.

### "We want a portal that intelligently routes and responds"
**How to scope:** Custom Platform with AI features embedded. One engagement, one team. The platform is the system of record and user interface; the AI handles the intelligent routing and response logic.

**How to position:** "This is a platform with smart capabilities built in — not two separate projects bolted together."

### "We're not sure what we need"
**How to scope:** Start with **Paid Discovery** ($15–25K). Two to three weeks of structured analysis where we map their current processes, quantify costs, and determine the right service mix. Discovery produces a documented recommendation with scope, timeline, and investment ranges.

**How to position:** "Let's spend two weeks understanding your situation before we recommend anything. The discovery has standalone value — you'll walk away with a documented picture of your current costs and a clear plan, even if you don't proceed with us."

## When to Walk Away — Universal Disqualifiers

*For universal objection responses (budget, vendor trust, core system migration, etc.), see Vicert-Services-Overview.md.*

Regardless of service, walk away (or defer) if:

- **No budget authority in the room.** If you can't get to someone who can approve spend, you're educating, not selling. Ask: "What's the approval process for a $200K investment?"
- **No defined pain — just curiosity.** "We're exploring options" without specific problems is a time sink. Respond: "We'd love to help. Let me know when a specific workflow or cost is keeping you up at night."
- **Active core system migration.** Timing is wrong for any significant build. Defer 6–12 months and stay in touch.
- **Hostile IT organization.** If IT is actively against external builds and leadership won't override, the project will die in security review. Find a different champion or move on.
- **They want a proposal without a discovery conversation.** "Just send me a proposal" without letting you understand their environment means they're collecting quotes for comparison, not seriously evaluating. Push for a discovery call: "I want to make sure any proposal reflects your actual situation — can we spend 30 minutes on your current setup?"

## Pricing Conversation Guidance

### When to Share Pricing
- **Do:** Share ranges early in the conversation. ("Typical builds in this range are $150–250K.") This qualifies budget fit before you invest in a full proposal.
- **Don't:** Give a specific number before discovery. ("This will be $175K." You don't know enough yet, and you've anchored to a number you may need to change.)
- **Don't:** Wait until the proposal to reveal pricing. Sticker shock at the proposal stage means you wasted both sides' time.

### How to Anchor
- **Anchor against current spend, not against zero.** "You're spending $400K/year today. We're proposing a $250K one-time investment with $50K/year ongoing. Year 2 savings alone cover the build."
- **Anchor against failed alternatives.** "The last time someone quoted you on this, what was the number? $1.2M? The same scope is $200–300K today."
- **Never anchor against the lowest possible price.** If you say "$150K" and discovery reveals it's a $250K project, you've lost credibility. Lead with the range.

### Handling "Just Send Me a Proposal"
This usually means one of three things:
1. **They're collecting comparison quotes.** Push for a discovery call to differentiate.
2. **They don't have decision authority and need something to show their boss.** Ask: "Who else would need to see this?"
3. **They're genuinely interested but busy.** Offer a lightweight 1-page summary with ranges, then schedule a full discovery.

## Creating Urgency — Timing Triggers

The content explains why Vicert's services are valuable. This section helps reps articulate why the prospect should act *now*.

### Budget Cycle Alignment
Healthcare organizations typically plan budgets in Q3–Q4 for the following year, with new budgets available in Q1.

- **If you're talking to a prospect in Q2–Q3:** Position for next year's budget. "Let's do paid discovery now ($15–25K from this year's budget) so you have a fully scoped proposal ready for next year's budget cycle."
- **If you're talking in Q4–Q1:** Propose a quick-start that uses remaining current-year budget or new-year budget. "If you have unspent budget from this year, a discovery engagement or pilot is an ideal use of it — you'll have results before year-end."

### Key-Person Departure
This is the single strongest urgency trigger for Workflow Automation.

- **The question to ask:** "The person who manages this process — are they within a few years of retirement? Any chance they might leave?"
- **The urgency framing:** "Every month without starting is a month of unmitigated institutional knowledge risk. If that person leaves before the system is built, you're not just losing a person — you're losing the process."
- **If they're already gone:** "You're already in recovery mode. Let's capture what's left of the institutional knowledge and build it into a system before more is lost."

### SaaS Contract Renewal Windows
Relevant for Custom Platforms prospects locked into vendor contracts.

- **The question to ask:** "When do your major SaaS contracts renew?"
- **The urgency framing:** "If we start the build 4–6 months before your renewal, you'll have a working alternative ready. That gives you negotiating leverage even if you decide to renew — and the option to migrate if you don't."
- **If renewal is imminent:** "Let's do a quick discovery engagement now. Even if the platform isn't ready for this renewal cycle, you'll have a concrete plan and budget for the next one."

### Regulatory Deadlines
CMS rules, state mandates, HEDIS submission deadlines, and interoperability requirements create hard "must be ready by X" drivers.

- **Examples:** CMS interoperability rule compliance, state-specific network adequacy reporting deadlines, HEDIS data submission windows, CMS directory accuracy mandates.
- **The urgency framing:** "The deadline is [date]. Working backward, we need to start by [date] to have a system in place. If we wait past [date], you're managing compliance manually for another cycle."

### Competitive Pressure
When competitors are moving to modern platforms or AI capabilities, the cost of waiting includes competitive disadvantage.

- **The urgency framing:** "Your competitors who adopt AI agents or modern platforms first will have a capability and cost advantage that compounds. In healthcare, where complexity is the defining characteristic, that advantage is especially durable."
- **When to use this:** With CEOs and GMs who think strategically about market position, not just operational cost.

## Engagement Progression — How Small Deals Become Big Ones

| Entry Point | Typical Size | Natural Next Step | Trigger for Next Step |
|-------------|-------------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Single workflow build | $100–175K | Additional workflows | "That worked great — we have three more processes like this" |
| Multi-workflow bundle | $200–350K | Custom platform consolidation | "Now that these workflows are automated, can we consolidate the tools too?" |
| Custom platform (Phase 1) | $200–300K | Platform expansion + workflow automation | "Phase 1 is working well — let's add Phase 2 and automate the manual processes around it" |
| AI Agent pilot | $50–80K | Production agent system | "The pilot proved the concept — let's scale it" |
| Discovery engagement | $15–25K | Any of the above | Discovery identifies the real problem and right service |
| Health-Tech: Technical discovery | $15–25K | MVP build or managed team | Discovery scopes the product build and validates technical approach |
| Health-Tech: MVP / first product build | $150–300K | Managed delivery team (ongoing) | "The MVP is working — we need to keep building" |
| Health-Tech: Managed delivery team | $30–50K/month | Team expansion or AI capability build | "We need more capacity" or "We need AI features in the product" |
| Health-Tech: Feature acceleration project | $100–250K | Managed team or additional projects | "We have a specific feature that needs to ship fast" |

*Key principle: Every engagement should end with a clear picture of what comes next. Don't just deliver — plant the seed for expansion.*

### Managing the Relationship Through Delivery

Closing the deal is step one. The expansion opportunity depends on staying engaged during delivery without overstepping the delivery team.

**When to start the expansion conversation:** After the first milestone delivery — when the client has seen working software and has confidence in the team. Not before. Bringing up "what's next" before they've seen results feels presumptuous. After they've seen results, it feels natural.

**Signals that indicate readiness for expansion:**
- "That worked great — we have three more processes like this." *(Direct ask — move to scoping immediately.)*
- "Can you do [adjacent capability]?" *(They're thinking beyond the original scope — explore.)*
- "My colleague in [other department] has a similar problem." *(Internal referral — ask for an introduction.)*
- End-of-Phase-1 review goes well and the client asks about Phase 2 unprompted. *(Natural expansion point — have a Phase 2 scope ready.)*

**How to stay engaged during delivery:**
- Attend milestone review meetings (as sales, not as delivery). Listen for expansion signals.
- Check in with the Engagement Manager monthly — not to interfere, but to understand how the client relationship is evolving.
- Don't sell during delivery. Your job during this phase is to listen, not pitch. The delivery team's success is the best sales tool you have.

**The end-of-Phase-1 moment:** This is the highest-leverage expansion point. The client has seen results, trusts the team, and understands the model. Come prepared with:
1. A summary of what was delivered and the impact it's having.
2. A recommended next step — "Based on what we learned in Phase 1, here's where the next highest-value opportunity is."
3. A rough scope and timeline for Phase 2 — not a full proposal, just enough to make the decision easy.

**What NOT to do:**
- Don't pressure for expansion if the client isn't satisfied with Phase 1. Fix Phase 1 first.
- Don't go around the delivery team. If the client raises a concern to you during delivery, route it to the Engagement Manager.
- Don't promise scope changes that the delivery team hasn't agreed to. Coordinate internally first.
