## Who This Is For (and Who It Isn't)

### Best-Fit Prospects

| Org Type | Why Custom Platforms Fit | Typical Entry Point |
|----------|--------------------------|---------------------|
| **HSOs (100–1,000 employees)** | Outgrown their initial SaaS stack, paying for 4–6 tools that overlap but leave gaps, can't justify enterprise platforms | Claims + eligibility consolidation |
| **Mid-size Payers (50K–300K members)** | Vendor "roadmap" features never arrive, locked into contracts for tools that cover 60% of needs | Member-facing portal + back-office consolidation |
| **MSOs / Management Services Orgs** | Managing multiple practice workflows across fragmented tools | Credentialing + referral management platform |
| **Specialty Health Plans (behavioral, dental, vision)** | Mainstream platforms built for medical don't fit their adjudication logic | Purpose-built claims platform for their specialty |

### Disqualification Criteria — Walk Away If:

- **They're happy with their SaaS stack.** If the pain is mild, the switching cost won't feel worth it. Look for active frustration, not theoretical improvement.
- **Total current SaaS spend is under $150K/year.** The ROI math doesn't close — the savings don't justify the build investment.
- **They have no internal IT capacity at all.** A custom platform needs someone on their side who can participate in requirements, manage infrastructure, and handle day-to-day admin. If they're fully outsourced with no technical staff, flag this as a risk.
- **They're locked into multi-year SaaS contracts with heavy exit penalties.** Qualify contract timelines early — if they're 18 months into a 3-year deal with a 6-figure buyout, the timing doesn't work. Revisit 6–12 months before renewal.
- **The "custom platform" they want is really an EHR replacement.** We don't build EHRs. If their primary pain is clinical documentation, this isn't our engagement.
- **They need real-time HL7/FHIR integration with 10+ external systems on day one.** Integration scope like this pushes timelines and budgets beyond our sweet spot. One or two integrations? Fine. Ten? That's a different project.

### Use Case 4: Value-Based Care Contract Management
Health plans and provider groups managing multiple value-based contracts need to track shared savings calculations, quality metrics, and provider incentive payments across different contract structures — each with its own formula, measurement period, and reconciliation process.

**Before:** Finance team manages VBC contracts in spreadsheets — one per contract. Shared savings calculations are manual, error-prone, and take weeks. Providers don't get timely performance feedback because the data isn't ready. Disputes at reconciliation are common because nobody trusts the numbers.
**After:** Unified platform handles multiple contract structures, auto-calculates shared savings against defined formulas, tracks quality metrics in real time, and generates provider scorecards. Reconciliation goes from weeks to days. Disputes drop because all parties work from the same data.

### Use Case 5: Unified Provider Network Management
Organizations managing provider networks deal with credentialing, contracting, directory maintenance, and performance tracking — typically in four separate systems that don't share data. The result is duplicated data entry, inconsistencies, and no single view of a provider's status across all dimensions.

**Before:** Credentialing in one system, contracts in another, directory in a third, performance data in a fourth. Staff manually cross-reference to answer basic questions like "Is this provider fully credentialed, under active contract, listed correctly in the directory, and meeting quality standards?" CMS directory accuracy requirements add compliance pressure.
**After:** Single platform where credentialing status, contract terms, directory data, and performance metrics are linked. Changes in one area automatically reflect in others. Staff answer provider status questions in seconds, not hours. Directory accuracy improves to meet CMS standards.

## Discovery Questions

**Opening questions (establish pain and spend):**
- How many SaaS platforms are you currently paying for across claims, eligibility, credentialing, and reporting?
- Do you have staff whose primary job is reconciling data between systems that don't talk to each other?
- What's your total annual spend across all these tools — licenses, plus the people managing the gaps?

**Deeper qualification (once pain is established):**
- Have you explored custom development before? What was the quote, and why did you walk away?
- Are there features your vendors have promised that have been "on the roadmap" for over a year?
- If you could consolidate to one system built exactly for your workflows, what would that change for your team?

**Technical qualification (with IT stakeholders):**
- Where does your data live today? What's the migration path from your current systems?
- What are your current SaaS contract timelines? When do renewals come up?
- What does your infrastructure look like — on-prem, cloud, hybrid? Any compliance requirements that dictate hosting?
- How many integrations with external systems (clearinghouses, state portals, payor connections) are must-haves on day one vs. can come in phase two?

**Quantification (get the numbers that feed the ROI math):**
- What's the fully loaded annual cost of the staff who spend significant time on reconciliation between systems? *(Feeds FTE cost line.)*
- What percentage of their time goes to reconciliation vs. their actual job function? *(Feeds % time allocation.)*
- Can you share your annual license costs for the SaaS tools involved? *(Feeds SaaS cost line — ask for actual invoices if they'll share.)*
- What was your last SaaS renewal increase, percentage-wise? *(Feeds compounding cost projection.)*
- What's your error rate on cross-system data, and what does an average error cost to fix? *(Feeds error remediation line.)*
- How many hours per month does your team spend on audit preparation related to these systems? *(Feeds compliance cost line.)*

*Use the prospect's answers to populate the Current State table in the ROI Math section. Prospect-provided numbers are far more persuasive than Vicert's ranges.*

### What's NOT in This Math (Be Honest About This)

- **SaaS contract buyout costs.** If they're mid-contract, there may be early termination fees. Qualify this early — it changes the payback timeline.
- **Parallel running period.** Plan for 1–3 months of running old and new systems simultaneously. This means ongoing SaaS costs during transition.
- **Internal IT time.** Someone on their team needs to participate in requirements, UAT, and go-live. Budget 20–30% of one FTE's time for 4–6 months.
- **Scope creep risk.** The $200–300K number assumes a disciplined Phase 1 scope. If the prospect wants "everything at once," the number goes up and the timeline extends. Manage this expectation early.
- **Future development.** The platform will need enhancements over time as requirements evolve. This isn't included in maintenance — it's separate project work.

## Competitive Positioning

### vs. Staying with SaaS (Healthedge, Cognizant TriZetto, Jiva, etc.)
SaaS gives you someone else's idea of how your business should work. When their assumptions don't match your reality — and in specialty plans, MSOs, and growing HSOs, they rarely do — you absorb the gap as manual labor. And you're locked into their release schedule, their priorities, and their annual price increases.

### vs. Big Consultancies (Cognizant, Deloitte, Accenture, Wipro)
For platform builds specifically: the compression from AI-accelerated engineering is highest on greenfield builds like this. A consultancy's $1.5–3M quote for a platform project becomes $200–300K because we compress scaffolding, testing, and boilerplate while our senior engineers handle architecture and domain logic. *(For universal positioning against big consultancies, see Vicert-Services-Overview.md.)*

### vs. Offshore Dev Shops
Cheaper hourly rates, but they lack healthcare domain expertise. Understanding adjudication logic, compliance frameworks, EDI standards, and clinical workflows isn't something you can offshore and learn on the fly. The projects that fail most often are the ones where the development team doesn't understand the domain.

### vs. Low-Code / No-Code Platforms (Appian, OutSystems, Salesforce Health Cloud)
These work until they don't. You'll get 70% of the way there quickly, then hit a wall on complex adjudication logic, custom integrations, or performance at scale. And you're still paying license fees — often substantial ones — for a platform you don't own.

### vs. Doing Nothing
Every month you wait costs you the difference between what you're spending and what you could be spending. That gap is $200–400K per year, plus the compounding effect of SaaS price increases and growing reconciliation burden.

## Buying Process Map

| Stakeholder | Their Concern | What They Need From You |
|-------------|--------------|------------------------|
| **CFO / VP Finance** (often the decision-maker) | Total cost of ownership, payback period, hidden costs | Honest ROI model with transition costs. Side-by-side: current annual spend vs. build + maintain. |
| **CIO / VP Technology** (often the blocker) | Maintenance burden, security, integration complexity, team bandwidth | Architecture overview, technology stack details, deployment model, maintenance SLA |
| **VP Operations** (often the champion) | Will this actually make my team's lives better? | Workflow walkthrough showing how their specific processes would work in the new system |
| **Compliance / Legal** | Data migration risk, regulatory compliance, vendor due diligence | BAA, security posture, compliance documentation, data migration plan |

**Typical timeline from first conversation to signed SOW:** 6–12 weeks. Larger orgs with formal procurement: 12–20 weeks.

**Common bottleneck:** CIO or IT leadership who see a custom platform as more work for their team. Address this proactively with the maintenance model and integration simplification narrative.

## Why We Lose — Learn From These

| Reason We Lost | What Happened | How to Prevent |
|----------------|--------------|----------------|
| **SaaS contract lock-in** | Prospect was interested but couldn't exit their current contracts without major penalties. | Qualify contract timelines in the first conversation. If renewal is 18+ months out, plan accordingly or find interim wins. |
| **CIO blocked it** | Operations loved it, but CIO didn't want "another system to manage" and didn't believe the reduced-maintenance narrative. | Get CIO in the room early. Show, don't tell — architecture walkthrough, not just a pitch deck. |
| **Scope ballooned** | Prospect wanted everything in Phase 1. Quote went from $250K to $500K+, timeline from 4 months to 9. They got sticker shock. | Hold the line on Phase 1 scope. Show the phase plan visually. "You get this in month 4, this in month 7, this in month 10." |
| **"We'll just build it ourselves"** | Internal dev team convinced leadership they could do it. (Rarely works, but takes 12+ months to learn that.) | Don't compete with their team. Position Vicert as acceleration: "Your team focuses on business logic and requirements, we handle the engineering velocity." |
| **Decision fatigue** | Too many stakeholders, no clear owner, meetings kept getting rescheduled. | Identify the decision-maker and the decision process early. If there isn't one, coach your champion. |

## When to Introduce Another Service

Listen for these signals during Custom Platform conversations — they point to additional opportunities:

- **Manual processes filling gaps between current tools.** Even after consolidating SaaS into a custom platform, some manual workflows will remain. Flag **Workflow Automation** for the processes the new platform won't replace — credentialing, compliance reporting, internal operations.
- **Document variation or judgment-based work.** If they mention processes that involve reading varied documents, handling exceptions that require interpretation, or making decisions with ambiguous inputs, **AI Agents** may be Phase 2 after the platform is in place.
- **"We want everything."** When the prospect wants full modernization, sequence it: **Platform first** (stable foundation), then **Workflow Automation** (formalize processes around it), then **AI Agents** (add intelligence to the parts that require judgment). Don't try to build all three simultaneously.
- **Health-tech company building a product.** If the prospect is a health-tech startup or product company that wants a platform for their own customers (not internal operations), the conversation overlaps with the **Health-Tech engagement model**. See Battlecard-Health-Tech.md.
