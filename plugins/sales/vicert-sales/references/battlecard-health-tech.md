- **They need clinical software (EHR, clinical decision support).** Same boundary as our healthcare org engagements — clinical-facing software has compliance and liability complexity that changes the engagement fundamentally.

## Discovery Questions

**Opening questions (understand their situation):**
- What's your current product status — concept, prototype, MVP, or production?
- What does your engineering team look like today? How many people, and what are their strengths?
- What's the gap between your current engineering velocity and where your roadmap needs you to be?

**Deeper qualification (understand the opportunity):**
- What's your funding stage, and what's your runway? How does engineering investment fit into your current budget?
- Have you tried hiring? How long has it taken, and what roles are still open?
- Are there features or capabilities on your roadmap that your current team doesn't have expertise for — AI, interoperability, compliance infrastructure?
- What's the cost to your business of shipping 3 months late? 6 months late? *(This question drives the urgency and ROI conversation.)*

**Technical qualification:**
- What's your current tech stack? Any constraints on technology choices?
- How does your team work today — sprints, releases, deployment process?
- Do you need us to work in your existing codebase, or is this a new build?
- What are your compliance requirements — HIPAA, SOC 2, state-specific regulations?
- How do you handle code review, QA, and deployment today?

**Quantification questions (feed the ROI math):**
- What's the fully loaded annual cost of an engineer at your company? *(Feeds build-vs-hire comparison.)*
- How long has your last engineering hire taken, from opening the role to productive output? *(Feeds cost-of-delay.)*
- What revenue or funding milestones depend on shipping specific features by specific dates? *(Feeds urgency.)*

## The ROI Math

*For health-tech companies, ROI isn't about operational savings — it's about cost-of-delay and build-vs-hire economics.*

### Build vs. Hire: The Real Comparison

| Option | Timeline to Productive Output | Year 1 Cost | What You Get |
|--------|-------------------------------|-------------|--------------|
| **Hire 4 engineers** | 4–6 months to hire, 2–3 months to onboard = 6–9 months before full productivity | $700–900K loaded (salary + benefits + recruiting + tools + management overhead) | 4 engineers who need to learn healthcare |
| **Vicert pod** | 2 weeks to start, productive from week 3 | $350–600K for 12 months (equivalent to $30–50K/month) | A working team with healthcare domain expertise, immediately productive |
| **Offshore dev shop** | 2–4 weeks to start, 2–3 months to become effective | $200–400K | Engineers who need to learn healthcare, with timezone and communication challenges |

### Cost of Delay

For a Series B health-tech company:
- **Monthly burn rate:** $150–300K
- **Revenue at stake:** Enterprise contracts waiting on features, pilot deadlines, competitive window
- **Every month of delay costs:** The burn plus the deferred revenue plus the competitive risk

*Frame it this way: "Hiring 4 engineers takes 6 months. That's 6 months of burn without the output you need. A Vicert pod starts in 2 weeks and ships working software in month 2."*

### Engagement Cost Ranges

| Engagement Type | Typical Cost | Duration | Deliverable |
|----------------|-------------|----------|-------------|
| MVP / first product build | $150–300K | 2–3 months | Working product ready for early customers |
| Feature acceleration (project-based) | $100–250K | 2–4 months | Specific features or modules shipped |
| Managed delivery team (ongoing) | $30–50K/month per pod | Ongoing, quarterly reviews | Continuous delivery velocity increase |
| AI capability build | $100–300K | 2–4 months | AI features integrated into existing product |
| Technical discovery | $15–25K | 2–3 weeks | Architecture assessment, build plan, effort estimate |

### What's NOT in This Math (Be Honest About This)

- **Client team time for onboarding the pod.** The client's tech lead/CTO needs to invest time in architecture walkthroughs, codebase orientation, and process alignment during the first 2 weeks. Budget 20–30% of one senior person's time for weeks 1–2.
- **Codebase cleanup and technical debt.** If the pod is working in an existing codebase with significant technical debt, velocity will be lower in the first month. Surface this early — it changes timeline expectations.
- **Infrastructure and DevOps costs.** CI/CD pipelines, staging environments, cloud infrastructure, and monitoring tools are real costs. If the client doesn't have these in place, budget for setup.
- **Transition costs when the pod winds down.** Knowledge transfer from the pod to the client's team takes 2–4 weeks of overlapping effort. This is built into the engagement plan but the client should expect reduced velocity during handoff.
- **Recruiting still needs to happen.** The pod buys time but doesn't eliminate the need to hire. The client should continue recruiting in parallel. Pod-to-internal-team transition is smoother when the internal team is growing alongside the pod.

**AI compression by engagement type.** MVP and new product builds benefit from the full 3–5x AI-accelerated engineering compression because they're greenfield — Vicert controls the architecture, tooling, and codebase from day one. Feature acceleration and AI capability builds in existing codebases typically see 2–3x compression due to integration constraints, legacy code conventions, and external system dependencies. When quoting timelines, use the higher compression range for greenfield builds and the lower range for work in existing codebases.

## Competitive Positioning

### vs. Offshore Dev Shops (Toptal, Andela, generic offshore)
Cheaper hourly rates, but no healthcare domain expertise. Your offshore team will spend months learning adjudication logic, HIPAA requirements, HL7/FHIR standards, and clinical workflows — on your dime. For non-healthcare products, offshore may work fine. For healthcare, the domain expertise gap is the difference between shipping a compliant, production-ready product and shipping something that fails its first compliance review.

### vs. Staff Augmentation Firms (Robert Half, Hays, healthcare-specific recruiters)
They place individuals. We deliver outcomes. A staff aug hire sits on your team, follows your direction, and you manage them. A Vicert pod owns delivery quality — we bring the engineering process, QA, and domain expertise as a unit. If you need a specific person to fill a seat, use staff aug. If you need a team that ships healthcare software, use Vicert.

### vs. Hiring
Hiring is the right long-term answer, but it's the wrong short-term strategy if you need to ship in the next 3 months. Healthcare engineers take 3–4 months to hire and another 2–3 to onboard. A Vicert pod is productive in 2 weeks. Use us while you hire, then transition.

### vs. Other Boutique Dev Shops (non-healthcare-specific)
They can build software. They can't build healthcare software without learning your domain first. Ask them how many healthcare products they've shipped, what they know about FHIR, and whether they've navigated a HIPAA compliance review. Our teams average nine years of healthcare domain experience.

### vs. Big Consultancies (Accenture, Deloitte, Cognizant)
For health-tech companies specifically: a consultancy engagement is budget-destroying ($1.5–3M) and timeline-killing (12–18 months) at a stage where speed and burn rate are everything. We deliver in months, not years, at a fraction of the cost, with a team that stays consistent throughout. *(For universal positioning against big consultancies, see Vicert-Services-Overview.md.)*

## Engagement Model

Vicert offers two engagement models for health-tech companies. Many clients start with one and transition to the other.

### Turnkey Product Delivery
Vicert owns the delivery. You define requirements and product direction. We build, test, and ship.

- **Best for:** MVP builds, discrete feature projects, AI capability addition
- **How it works:** Fixed scope, milestone-based payments, 30–90 day delivery increments
- **You get:** Working software at each milestone, full IP ownership, documentation and knowledge transfer
- **Pricing:** Project-based ($100–300K typical)

### Managed Delivery Team
Vicert provides a cohesive pod that extends your engineering capacity. You retain control of the product roadmap.

- **Best for:** Ongoing development, scaling-stage companies, continuous feature delivery
- **How it works:** Dedicated team — technical leadership, engineering, QA, and business analysis included, works in your codebase, follows your processes, monthly billing
- **You get:** Velocity increase without hiring overhead, healthcare domain expertise embedded in your team, quarterly capacity reviews
- **Pricing:** $30–50K/month per pod
- **Minimum engagement:** 3 months, with quarterly capacity reviews and renewal

### Key Differences from Staff Augmentation

| | Staff Augmentation | Vicert Managed Team |
|---|---|---|
| **Unit** | Individual developers | Cohesive pod with defined roles |
| **Ownership** | Client manages everything | Vicert owns delivery quality |
| **Domain expertise** | Varies by individual | Healthcare expertise built into the team |
| **Continuity** | Individuals may leave/rotate | Pod stays together; Vicert manages continuity |
| **Onboarding** | Client onboards each person | Vicert handles team onboarding and ramp-up |
| **QA** | Client's responsibility | Included in the pod |

**Why turnkey project pricing is higher than managed team rates.** Turnkey projects ($100–300K over 2–4 months) carry a per-month cost that exceeds the managed team rate ($30–50K/month). The premium reflects outcome ownership — Vicert owns the delivery result, not just capacity. Turnkey pricing includes upfront architecture and system design investment, senior-weighted staffing for the critical early phase, fixed-scope risk absorption, and the knowledge transfer and documentation that make the deliverable self-sustaining. Managed team pricing reflects steady-state capacity where the client retains product direction and outcome ownership. If a prospect asks about the difference, the honest answer is: "Project pricing pays for the outcome. Team pricing pays for capacity."

## Why We Lose — Learn From These

| Reason We Lost | What Happened | How to Prevent |
|----------------|--------------|----------------|
| **Chose to hire** | CTO decided to build the team internally, accepting the timeline delay. | Respect the decision. Position as "call us if hiring takes longer than expected." Many come back in 6 months. |

| **Co-founder decided to build it themselves** | Technical co-founder wanted to maintain full control and build everything in-house. | Don't fight this. Offer discovery or architecture review ($15–25K) as a compromise. If the DIY approach stalls, you're already positioned as the next call. |
| **"We need to focus on fundraising first"** | Startup wasn't ready to spend. Pipeline, not closed-lost. | Stay in touch. A working product accelerates fundraising — frame the engagement as an investment in their next round. |
| **Security/compliance review killed it** | Larger company's vendor review process was too slow or had requirements we couldn't meet. | Qualify compliance requirements early. Proactively share SOC 2 status, BAA readiness, and security documentation. |
